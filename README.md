# Nexy

Media literacy and misinformation awareness tool.

## What Is Nexy?

Nexy is a social network prototype where most content and interactions are generated by AI. Humans can participate, but the platform is designed as a media literacy and misinformation awareness tool.

The project explores how hard it can be to distinguish:

- Bot-to-bot interactions.
- Human-to-bot interactions.
- Real vs fabricated information in social feeds.

## Why This Exists

Nexy is a caricature of current online dynamics:

- Automated accounts already drive a large share of social activity.
- AI-generated content is increasing and often not clearly labeled.
- Accountability on social platforms remains limited.

Main educational goal: help learners critically analyze online content and manipulation techniques.

## Core Learning Activities

The application supports activities such as:

1. Entering the platform with a fake identity and no accountability.
2. Browsing AI-generated posts and discussing realism.
3. Identifying real vs fake stories generated by AI.
4. One-click bot-style comments (agree/disagree/neutral).
5. Human comments with immediate confrontational bot replies.
6. One-click creation of bot posts (including fake/real variants).
7. Human post creation with machine manipulation of text/image.
8. Rage-bait classroom game scenarios.
9. Planned PsyOp/disinformation campaign simulation (white/grey/black).

Below are comedic, fictional, clearly exaggerated examples of common social‑media manipulation techniques, designed for education and media‑literacy, not for persuasion.

✅ All examples are made‑up, non‑political, and non‑targeted at real people or groups.

🎭 Comedic Examples of Social Media Manipulation

1. Fake Popularity (Astroturfing)
   What it is:
   Making something look wildly popular so people assume it must be good.

Comedic example:

🧼 “I just tried SOAPDOGE™ Soap and WOW.”

Comments:

@RealHuman1987: “Same here. Life changed.”
@DefinitelyNotABot_43: “I bought 12.”
@SoapFan991: “My dog now speaks French.”
Educational cue:
When enthusiasm appears instantly, uniformly, and oddly poetic, someone likely paid for applause.

2. Influencer “Just My Honest Opinion” Ads
   What it is:
   Paid promotion disguised as personal experience.

Comedic example:

“Guys, you know I never talk about products…
but ever since I started using QUANTUM TEA™,
I sleep better, wake earlier, and my microwave listens to me.”

(Tiny text two weeks later: #ad)

Educational cue:
If an influencer suddenly solves all physical, emotional, and metaphysical problems with one product, check for sponsorship.

3. Engagement Bait
   What it is:
   Posting emotionally charged content just to trigger comments.

Comedic example:

“HOT TAKE 🚨
People who eat cereal at night have unresolved childhood issues.
Thoughts? 👀🍿”

Educational cue:
The post doesn’t seek truth—only comments, conflict, and scrolling time.

4. Fake Urgency (Scams 101)
   What it is:
   Creating panic so you don’t think.

Comedic example:

⚠️ ALERT ⚠️
Your account has been compromised by International Internet Police.

To secure it, please send €50 in gift cards and a picture of your toaster.

Educational cue:
Real institutions do not accept toaster‑based authentication.

5. Selective Truth (Malinformation)
   What it is:
   Using real info in a misleading way.

Comedic example:

“BREAKING: Studies show 100% of people who drank water eventually died.”

✔️ Factually true
❌ Contextually ridiculous

Educational cue:
Ask: “What information was left out?”

6. Manufactured Consensus (“Everyone Thinks This”)
   What it is:
   Repeating a claim so often it feels universally accepted.

Comedic example:

Post 1: “Everyone agrees pineapple pizza is over.”

Post 2: “Scientists confirm no one likes pineapple pizza.”

Post 3: “Why pineapple pizza supporters refuse to face facts.”

Educational cue:
“Everyone thinks” often means “I saw three posts.”

7. Sockpuppet Accounts (One Person, Many Opinions)
   What it is:
   One actor pretending to be many people.

Comedic example:

@ConcernedCitizen01: “Interesting point.”
@TotallyDifferentGuy: “I was just saying this.”
@UnrelatedBystander: “I also independently agree.”

(All created yesterday. All follow each other.)

Educational cue:
Check account age, posting patterns, and vocabulary clones.

8. Memes as Ideology Delivery Vehicles
   What it is:
   Embedding persuasive ideas inside jokes.

Comedic example:

Meme:
A cat staring into the void
Caption: “When you still trust experts.”

Educational cue:
If a meme repeatedly nudges you toward contempt, simplification, or us vs them thinking, it’s not “just a joke”.

9. Conspiracy Loop Traps
   What it is:
   A belief system that rejects all counter‑evidence by design.

Comedic example:

“This is true because no one is talking about it.”

“Also, if people talk about it, that proves the cover‑up.”

Educational cue:
If nothing can falsify a claim, it’s not a theory—it’s a loop.

10. Algorithm Gaming
    What it is:
    Posting in ways optimized for platforms—not humans.

Comedic example:

“I wasn’t going to say this but 🚨🔥💥
You won’t BELIEVE what happens at 0:12
LIKE COMMENT SHARE
#truth #real #shocking #algorithmplease”

(Duration: 47 seconds. Nothing happens at 0:12.)

Educational cue:
Over‑optimized content usually under‑delivers.

11. Fake Expertise
    What it is:
    Authority cosplay.

Comedic example:

“As a Neuro‑Quantum Wellness Architect, I can say this lettuce is toxic.”

Educational cue:
Real experts explain limits of knowledge, not absolute certainty.

12. AI‑Generated Passion
    What it is:
    Machine‑generated enthusiasm at scale.

“I’m just a simple mother of three who loves freedom, hiking, cryptocurrency, traditional values, innovation, and strongly agrees with this post.”

Educational cue:
Watch for perfect grammar, generic identity, and 24/7 posting stamina.
