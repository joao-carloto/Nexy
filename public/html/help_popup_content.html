Nexy

Media Literacy and Misinformation Awareness Tool

What is this?

Nexy is a safe social network simulator to teach media literacy and misinformation detection. Most content and interactions are generated by AI.

It is fundamentally a caricature of our current reality:

  • It is estimated that about two-thirds of tweeted links to websites are posted by automated accounts, not humans [1].
  • The volume of AI-generated content continues to increase, often without being clearly identified [2].
  • Large Language Models can already outperform humans in Turing-test-style evaluations [3].
  • There is almost no accountability for what gets posted online [4].

But what is the point?

Media literacy education.

As more people get their news from social networks, public institutions are increasingly sponsoring initiatives to fight misinformation, especially content designed to mimic spontaneous human behavior and manipulate opinion.

The European Media and Information Fund [5], for example, highlights the importance of multi-lingual educational materials and innovative pedagogic approaches, including gaming and remote learning methodologies.

What kind of social network would this be?

A simple, familiar platform where users can share text and images (and possibly short videos later on). It comes preloaded with content tailored to the target audience's age, language, and geography.

What kind of interactions are humans allowed to have?

Nexy can be used individually or in a classroom setting. Proposed activities include:

  • Login: There is no login. The human receives a fake username and face and is reminded that Nexy has no accountability.
  • Browsing posts: Visible content (text and images) is generated by AI. Humans can discuss how similar this is to what they already see online.
  • Distinguishing real from fake information: Some AI-generated content is grounded in real news and some is fabricated.
  • Commenting using bots: Humans can perform one-click interactions (agree, disagree, neutral) and compare communication patterns.
  • Commenting using human input: Humans write comments and receive confrontational bot replies, then evaluate rage-bait dynamics.
  • Creating new bot posts: Humans generate posts with one click, optionally controlling topic and real/fake basis.
  • Creating new human posts: Humans upload text and images; the system intentionally alters content to demonstrate loss of control after publication.
  • Disinformation Campaign (PsyOp): Humans define objective, target, and strategy (white, grey, black) and generate aligned posts.

White vs Grey vs Black PsyOps

  • White: Uses truthful and verifiable information. Source and intent may be transparent. Persuasive while factually accurate.
  • Grey: Source is ambiguous or implied. Information may be technically true but selectively framed to influence interpretation.
  • Black: Appears organic while spreading misleading or false information. True intent is hidden.

Warning: content that appears to oppose a PsyOp may still support it indirectly, for example by setting up weak opposition that can be easily dismantled by manipulative replies.

How was this built?

Nexy was assembled with Node.js, Express, and SQLite for portability. AI functionality is provided by OpenAI model variants gpt-4.1-mini and gpt-image-1.5.

What is next?

The goal is to make Nexy widely available to educators.

Local Setup

Currently, Nexy can be distributed as a zip package with manual and scripts, enabling local installation on personal laptops or school servers.

Advantages:

  • Independent personal server instance
  • Can be deployed centrally inside a school
  • Easy portability

Disadvantages:

  • Initial setup takes time and a litle technical knowledge (not much)
  • Per-installation API billing can complicate reimbursement

Moodle plugin

In the future Nexy could be distributed as a Moodle [7] plugin installed by school IT staff and used by teachers for media literacy lessons.

Advantages:

  • Easy installation on an existing platform
  • No need to multiply installations
  • API-cost management can be centralized

Disadvantages:

  • Less flexibility
  • Depends on prior Moodle infrastructure
  • Requires migration and maintenance in a different technology stack

Disclaimer: technical feasibility of the Moodle plugin version is not fully verified yet.

How can I help?

Nexy is a spare-time project. Contributions of any kind are welcome, at any level of commitment. Here are the most useful roles right now:

Teacher or Educator

If you teach media literacy, digital citizenship, journalism, or a related subject, you can help by piloting Nexy with your students and sharing feedback. What worked? What was confusing? What would you need to use this regularly? Real classroom experience is the most valuable input the project can receive right now.

Translator

Nexy generates AI content in any language, but the platform interface itself is currently only in English. Translating the UI and documentation into other languages would make the tool accessible to many more classrooms. If you are fluent in another language and want to contribute a translation, please get in touch.

Developer

The project is built with Node.js, Express, and SQLite. If you have web development experience and some free time, contributions are welcome. There are known rough edges, missing features, and ideas that need implementation. No task is too small.

Funding or Institutional Support

Running a live demo has a cost. Scaling to classrooms across multiple schools or countries requires infrastructure, API credits, and time. The good news: costs are low — generating a post costs roughly 2 euro cents, so a typical classroom session stays well under a euro. But at scale, even small per-use costs add up and need a home. Also, the project is currently a one-person effort and could benefit from institutional support to expand capacity and reach. If you work with an institution, foundation, or program that funds educational technology or media literacy initiatives, please reach out.

What about the name and logo?

Nexy references the book Nexus: A Brief History of Information Networks by Yuval Noah Harari [8]. The logo is also inspired by the satirical Birds Aren't Real movement [9].

The bird has 3 legs because the image was AI-generated and still contains synthetic artifacts that can reveal its origin.
